## **1. The Philosophical Problem**
For a long time, consciousness was only a topic for philosophers.

However, after the pioneering work by Crick & Koch (1990) (who suggested studying the neural correlates of consciousness), consciousness gained significant attention in the scientific community. Since then, many theories have been proposed (for a review, see Seth & Bayne, 2022).

Philosophy, its ideas, and its definitions shape how we do research.

### **A. -isms**
In *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness*, Anil Seth (2021) summarizes the -isms as:

- **Functionalism:** Consciousness depends on the functions performed by the brain, not on its physical structure.
- **Materialism:** Consciousness arises from physical processes in the brain.
- **Dualism:** Consciousness and physical reality are separate things.
- **Panpsychism:** Consciousness is a fundamental property of all matter.
- **Idealism:** Consciousness is the primary reality, and the physical world emerges from it.
- **Illusionism:** Consciousness is an illusion; it doesn‚Äôt exist as we experience it.

### **B. The Hard and the Easy Problem(s)**
David Chalmers (1995) argued that consciousness has two problems: the easy ones and the hard one.

- **Easy problems:** Finding the neural substrates for conscious perception, memory, and other behaviors.
- **Hard problem:** Explaining how the brain, a physical thing, creates a subjective experience.

Nagel (1980) proposed a smart and popular thought experiment to understand this problem:

> **What is it like to be a bat?**

Nagel argues that trying to imagine what the bat feels is incorrect. The correct answer is what the bat feels **as a bat**.

### **C. Phenomenological vs. Access Consciousness**
Block (1995) suggested distinguishing different kinds of consciousness:

- **Access consciousness:** The sensory information that becomes conscious.
- **Phenomenal consciousness:** Pure subjective experience.

The **hard problem** is explaining phenomenal consciousness, while **easy problems** are related to access consciousness.

### **D. Illusionism**
Illusionists deny the hard problem entirely.

For them, subjective experiences are **not mysterious**; they are just illusions our brains create (Frankish, 2016).

Some influential thinkers in this view are:
- **Daniel Dennett**
- **Keith Frankish**

## **2. How Neuroscientists Do Research On Consciousness?**
How neuroscientists study consciousness depends on **how they define it**.

Some classic experimental methods include:

### **1. Binocular Rivalry**
This occurs when each eye is shown a different image simultaneously.

- The brain alternates between them, making perception switch from one image to the other.
- This helps study the **neural correlates of conscious perception**.

### **2. Priming**
Priming occurs when exposure to one stimulus influences the response to another.

- Often, this happens without conscious awareness.
- Neuroscientists use this to explore **unconscious vs. conscious perception**.

### **3. Dreams**
Dreams provide a **natural state of altered consciousness**.

- Siclari et al. (2017) used **high-definition EEG** to predict dream content.
- They identified a **posterior cortical hot zone** activated when dreaming.

### **4. Other Methods**
- **The mirror test** (Boyle, 2017)
- **Psychedelics & altered states** (Yaden et al., 2021)
- **Metacognition experiments** (Fleming, 2023)
- **Illusions, such as the Rubber Hand Illusion** (Constantini & Haggard, 2007)

## **3. Two Theories of Consciousness**
Over the last decades, two main theories have dominated the field:

### **A. Integrated Information Theory (IIT)**
First proposed by **Giulio Tononi (2004)**, IIT suggests that **consciousness is integrated information**.

- It proposes a way to **measure** consciousness with **Phi**.
- It defines **five axioms** of experience: **intrinsicality, information, integration, exclusion, and composition** (Albantakis, 2023).
- The main problem? **Phi is impossible to falsify**.

### **B. Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)**
Proposed by **Baars (1997, 2005)** and later refined by **Dehaene & Naccache (2001)**.

- **Consciousness happens when information enters the "global workspace" of the brain.**
- GNWT is **well-supported** by research (Mashour et al., 2020).
- However, **it focuses on access consciousness** rather than phenomenal consciousness.

Other theories include:
- **Higher Order Theory (HOT)** (LeDoux & Brown, 2017)
- **Prefrontal Perceptual Reality Monitoring (PRM)** (Lau, 2021)
- **Beast Machine Theory** (Seth & Tsakiris, 2018)

For a **review of 22 theories**, see **Seth & Bayne (2022).**
